What is R-squared (RÂ²)?

How good is our prediction line?

What Does RÂ² Tell Us? ðŸ¤”

Imagine you made a scatter plot showing how many hours students studied ($x$) and their test scores ($y$). You then drew the "line of best fit" (the regression line) that tries to predict the score based on study hours.

R-squared (written as $R^2$) is like a "grade" for your prediction line. It tells you what percentage (%) of the difference in test scores can be explained by the difference in study hours.

Think about it: Why do students get different scores?

  • One reason might be that they studied for different amounts of time. $R^2$ tells us how much of the score difference is because of study time. (This is the explained part).
  • But there are other reasons too! Maybe some students slept well, some are naturally better at the subject, some were lucky, some were unlucky. These other reasons cause differences in scores that our study time line can't explain. (This is the unexplained part or "error").

$R^2$ measures how much of the story (the difference in scores) is told by the factor we looked at (study time).

Reading the RÂ² Grade ðŸŽ¯

$R^2$ is always a number between 0 and 1, which we usually talk about as a percentage (0% to 100%).

$R^2 = 0.90$ (90%)

Meaning: Wow! 90% of the reason scores are different is because study hours were different. Our line is a great predictor!

$R^2 = 0.50$ (50%)

Meaning: Okay. 50% of the score differences are explained by study hours. The other 50% is due to other stuff. The line is an okay predictor.

$R^2 = 0.10$ (10%)

Meaning: Hmm. Only 10% of the score difference is related to study hours. Most of the difference is because of other things. This line isn't a very good predictor.

  • An $R^2$ of 1.0 (100%) means your line is a perfect predictor. All the dots are exactly on the line. Knowing $x$ lets you know $y$ perfectly.
  • An $R^2$ of 0 (0%) means your line is useless. Knowing the study hours tells you nothing about the likely test score.
  • A higher $R^2$ means your line "fits" the dots better. What counts as "good" depends on what you're studying. Sometimes even a low $R^2$ can be interesting!

The Link Between $r$ and $R^2$ (It's Easy!)

Remember the correlation coefficient ($r$)? It tells you how strong the linear relationship is (from -1 to +1).

For simple predictions (just one $x$ predicting one $y$), $R^2$ is simply $r$ multiplied by itself!

$R^2 = r \times r = r^2$

  • If the correlation $r$ is strong (like +0.8 or -0.8), squaring it gives a high $R^2$ (like 0.64 or 64%). A strong relationship means the line fits well!
  • If the correlation $r$ is weak (like +0.2 or -0.2), squaring it gives a very low $R^2$ (like 0.04 or 4%). A weak relationship means the line fits poorly.
  • Since squaring a negative number makes it positive, $R^2$ is always between 0 and 1.

Simple Analogy: Explaining Plant Growth ðŸŒ±

Imagine you're trying to explain why some tomato plants ($y$) grew taller than others.

  • You measure how much water ($x$) each plant got and find a decent relationship. You make a prediction line and calculate $R^2 = 0.70$.
  • This means 70% of the difference in plant heights can be explained by the difference in how much water they received.
  • The other 30% (100% - 70%) is due to other things you didn't measure (sunlight, soil quality, type of tomato, etc.). This is the unexplained part.

$R^2$ tells you how good your chosen factor (water) is at explaining the outcome (height).
