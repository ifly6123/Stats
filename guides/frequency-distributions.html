Frequency Distributions & Histograms

Making sense of messy data by organizing it!

Why Organize Data? 🤔

Imagine you ask 30 classmates how many hours they spent studying last week. You might get a jumble of numbers like this (this is called raw data):

3, 5, 2, 7, 4, 5, 6, 3, 8, 4, 5, 6, 2, 7, 5, 4, 6, 8, 3, 5, 4, 7, 6, 5, 4, 9, 3, 5, 6, 4

Looking at this list, it's hard to quickly understand how much studying is typical, or if anyone studied a lot or a little. We need a way to organize it!

What is a Frequency Distribution? 📋

A frequency distribution is like sorting your data into bins or categories and then counting how many data points fall into each bin. The frequency is simply the count.

It helps us see patterns, like:

  • Which values occur most often?
  • How spread out is the data?
  • Are there any unusual values (outliers)?

Grouping Data: Creating Classes

Sometimes, especially with lots of different numbers (like heights or weights - called continuous data), it's useful to group values into classes or intervals. For data with fewer, distinct options (like the number of siblings - called discrete data), you might just count each value separately.

Let's group our study hours data:

  1. Find the Range: The highest value minus the lowest value.
    Formula: $Range = Maximum Value - Minimum Value$
    Example: Max = 9, Min = 2. Range = 9 - 2 = 7 hours.
  2. Choose Number of Classes (Groups): Choose a reasonable number of groups, often between 5 and 15. Let's pick 4 classes.
  3. Determine Class Width: How "wide" each group should be.
    Approx. Formula: $Class Width \approx \frac{Range}{\text{Number of Classes}}$ (Round up to a convenient number).
    Example: 7 / 4 = 1.75. Let's round up to a width of 2 hours.
  4. Set Class Limits: Create the bins. Make sure they don't overlap and cover the whole range.
    Example Classes: 2-3 hours, 4-5 hours, 6-7 hours, 8-9 hours.
  5. Tally and Count: Go through your data and count how many values fall into each class. This count is the frequency.

  • Relative Frequency: The proportion of the data in a class (Frequency / Total Count). Often shown as a percentage.
    Formula: $Relative Frequency = \frac{\text{Class Frequency (f)}}{\text{Total Count (n)}}$
  • Cumulative Frequency: The total number of observations up to the end of that class.

The Histogram: A Picture of Frequency 📊

A histogram is the best way to *see* your frequency distribution. Think of it as a bar chart for grouped numbers.

  • The Horizontal Axis (X-axis) represents the data values or the class intervals (the "bins"). The numbers should cover the full range of your data.
  • The Vertical Axis (Y-axis) represents the frequency (the count) or sometimes the relative frequency (percentage) for each bin.
  • Bars Touch: The bars usually touch each other to show that the data scale is continuous or grouped into adjacent intervals. There are no gaps between 17cm and 18cm, for example.

Choosing the Right Number of Bins is Important!

The number of bins (classes) you choose can change how the histogram looks and what story it tells. Look at these examples using the same data but different bin widths:

[Image comparing histogram with few bins vs many bins]
  • Too Few Bins: Can hide important details and make the distribution look like a block.
  • Too Many Bins: Can create a "spiky" histogram that makes it hard to see the overall shape due to small variations.

There's no single perfect number, but starting with 5-15 bins is often a good approach. You can try different numbers to see which best reveals the underlying pattern.

Interpreting the Shape

The histogram's shape gives you quick insights:

  • Symmetrical (Bell-Shaped): Data clusters in the middle and tails off evenly on both sides. (Example: Heights of many people).
  • Skewed Right (Positively Skewed): Most data is on the left, with a long tail stretching to the right. (Example: Income – most people earn less, a few earn much more).
  • Skewed Left (Negatively Skewed): Most data is on the right, with a long tail stretching to the left. (Example: Scores on a very easy test – most people do well).
  • Bimodal: Has two distinct peaks. This often means you have two different groups mixed together in your data. (Example: Heights of adults if you mix men and women).
  • Uniform: All bins have roughly the same frequency. (Example: Rolling a fair die many times - each number should appear about equally often).
