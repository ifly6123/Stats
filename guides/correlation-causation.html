Correlation vs. Causation ‚ö†Ô∏è

Just because two things happen together doesn't mean one caused the other!

The BIGGEST Rule in Statistics!

This is probably the most important idea to remember when looking at data: Correlation does NOT imply causation!

What does this mean?

  • Correlation just means two things seem to happen together or move in a related way (like we saw with the correlation coefficient, $r$).
  • Causation means that one thing *directly causes* another thing to happen (like flipping a light switch *causes* the light to turn on).

It's super tempting to think that if two things are strongly correlated, one must be causing the other. But often, that's wrong!

The Classic Example: Ice Cream Sales and Crime Rates üç¶üöì

Imagine you collect data over a year and make a scatter plot. You find a strong positive correlation (maybe $r = +0.7$) between the amount of ice cream sold each month and the number of crimes reported each month.

Does this mean eating ice cream CAUSES crime? Of course not! That sounds silly.

Does committing crimes CAUSE people to want ice cream? Probably not!

So, what's going on? There's usually a **hidden reason** (a third variable) that's affecting *both* things. This hidden reason is called a confounding variable or a lurking variable.

What's the Hidden Reason Here?

In this case, the confounding variable is **Hot Weather (Temperature)**.

  • When it's hot, more people buy ice cream.
  • When it's hot, more people are outside, maybe leaving windows open, and sometimes tempers flare more easily, which can lead to an increase in crime.

The hot weather causes both ice cream sales AND crime rates to go up together. They are correlated, but one doesn't cause the other. The heat is the real driver.

Why is This So Important?

Mistaking correlation for causation leads to bad decisions and silly conclusions!

  • You might see a correlation between kids who play video games and kids who get good grades. Does playing games *cause* good grades? Probably not. Maybe kids who have more free time (because they finish homework quickly) do both?
  • You might see a correlation between the number of storks in a town and the number of babies born. Do storks deliver babies? No! Towns with more people tend to have both more houses (good for stork nests) and more babies. The **town size** is the confounding variable.

Always ask: Is there another reason these two things might be happening together? What's the hidden story?
